**Procedurally Generated, Low-Poly Planets** **John Freeman (jcf1@williams.edu), Jose Rivas (jdr3@williams.edu),** **Julia Goldman (jdg2@williams.edu),** **and Matheus de Carvalho Souza (mc13@williams.edu)** ![A unique, procedually generated, low-poly planet.](teaser.png border=1) Introduction ============================================================================================ Our project was to procedurally generate low-poly planets using triangle meshes and noise functions. Through the use of different noise functions, textures, object placements, destruction and bevelling algorithms, and height ratios, we can generate an infinite number of low-poly planets that are structurally diverse and awe-inspiring, despite their lack of realism. In fact, it is their lack of realism that makes low-poly planets so aesthetically pleasing. By applying different styles to them, especially cartoon-like shading and coloring, we can create planets that look like they come straight out of medieval fantasy. In this lab, we created procedurally generated our planets through the use of three different spherical heightfields placed on top of each other. Each heightfield had a different noise applied to it, which added features such as rippling water, the appearance and disappearance of land masses, and mountains. By adding further parameters, we can further improve our results. For example, texture mapping, the decimation of triangles through edge collapsing, and bevelling all allow for improved shading and planet contours. The addition of different entities, such as trees, birds, and clouds, and the addition of animation further gives each planet more diversity and brings them to life before the user's eyes. All of this comes together to give the user limitless capabilities to create stunning and diverse universes. Have a dragon model that can orbit a planet Topic Overview ===================================================================================== Heightfields ----------------------------------------------------------- The planets are represented through a combination of layered, spherical heightfields. From top to bottom, these spheres represent the mountains, land, and water planes of our planet. We generate these spheres using the icosagon tesselation algorithm [#Larsen] and apply the algorithm recursively for some number of times to generate more detailed spheres. We then apply appply noise mapping, decimation, and bevelling algorithms to each sphere to create the base of our planet. ![Example of a sphere after 5 recursions](5-sphere.png width=400px) ![Wireframe of a sphere 5 recursions](5-sphere-wire.png width=400px) ![Example of a simple planet](planet-layers-together.png height=90px width=160px) ![Water plane of the planet](water-layer.png height=90px width=160px) ![Land plane of the planet](land-layer.png height=90px width=160px) ![Mountain plane of the planet](mountain-layer.png height=90px width=160px) Noise ----------------------------------------------------------- We use Perlin noise [#Perlin] to generate a series of 2-dimensional images that are used to generate the heights of our three heightfields and place object on and around our planet. Each field has it's own noise image that is used to gererate it's heights by mapping the image to the sphere and raising the vertex based on the pixel value of the image it cooresponds to. The water and land heightfields are based on one noise function applied to their respective images, but the mountain plane is a composite image based on three seperate noise functions being applied to the same image, which results in more interesting and complex mountain ranges for our planets. ![Example of noise used for planet water](noiseExampleOcean.png width=200px) ![Example of noise used for planet land](noiseExampleLand.png width=200px) ![Example of noise used for planet mountains](noiseExampleMount.png width=200px) Meshes ---------------------------------------------------------- The heightfields are stored as meshes. A decimation algorithm is used to simplify the mesh. Then Beveling adds rectangles between mesh triangles to smooth out corners. Our mesh has functions that allow us to easily generate object files or articulated models from our meshes with appropriate normals and texture coordinates for texture mapping. Decimation Algorithms ---------------------------------------------------------- Decimation algorithms are used to simplify meshes by reducing the number of faces while still keeping its underlying shape and topology. The decimation algorithm we chose is edge collapsing as outlined by [#Stanford_Graphics] and [#Shertler], which consists of taking the $n$ least important edges away from the mesh, which effectifely removes its adjacent faces. For our purposes we chose the least important edge to be the edge whose angle between its adjacent face normals is closest to 180 degrees, weighted by the edge length. The main purpose of edge collapsing in our project, however, was not to merely simplify the mesh, but to make the low polyness aesthetic of our planets more evident by making the polygon subdivision less uniform. An edge collapsed sphere: ![Original Sphere](2016-12-07_065_starter_r2849_g3d_r981__original_sphere.jpg width=150px) ![2000 edges collapsed](2016-12-07_066_starter_r2849_g3d_r981__2000_edges_collapsed.jpg width=150px) ![4000 edges collapsed](2016-12-07_067_starter_r2849_g3d_r981__4000_edges_collapsed.jpg width=150px) ![8000 edges collapsed](2016-12-07_068_starter_r2849_g3d_r981__8000_edges_collapsed.jpg width=150px) Bevelling ----------------------------------------------------------- In order to create a smooth transition between triangles, we used a bevelling function. This functions shrinks each triangle face by a specified amount, moving the vertices of each triangles towards the triangle's center, causing each face to become disconnected from one another. It then fills in the missing spaces with rectangles, reconnecting the triangles to keep the mesh air tight. The normal of each vertex is artificially set to the normal of the face it is part of to make the bevelling more apparent. ![A planet with shrunken faces](beveling1.jpg width=300px)![A beveled surface](beveling.jpg width=300px)![Close-up shot](bevelingclose.jpg width=300px) The result is a more cartoon-like feel for the planet, because it gets rid of extremely sharp edges to allow for better shading. Animation ------------------------------------------------------------ The animation was done using G3D's entity tracking feature ([G3D::Entity::Track](http://g3d.cs.williams.edu/g3d/G3D10/build/manual/class_g3_d_1_1_entity_1_1_track.html#ae2e76f1d9cae33a68b4d6e1db947a364])), which allows for indirect manipulation of each entity. These tracks are created by expanding a BNF grammar that describes the various possible transformations for the entity's position. The expanded grammar creates a new 4D matrix for each frame that is applied to the entity's position. For example, the water field, as the bottom-most layer of the planet, has a simple track causing it to orbit around its own axis. On the other hand, the birds and dragon follow a much more complex track, which starts by applying a rotation matrix to each entity's position, applying a transformation to make it look towards its next position, applying another transformation to make it orbit the planet, and finally a final transformation to rotate the entity's orbit along the x axis. Finally, the tracking feature allows an entity to depend on another entity to determine its position. As an example, the upper two layers of the planet depend on the water layer to determine their next position. This was especially useful when creating moon's and satellites, because only the water field needs to calculate the orbit, and the other two layers simply follow it. ![Ice video](2016-12-07_056_starter_r2827_g3d_r981__Barbie_s_ice_planet_with_clouds_video.mp4 width = 550px) Design ===================================================================================== The following is a description of our design. ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ [Planet](class_planet.html) is responsible for building the spheres that make up our different heightfields, map noise onto the sphere, and allows for the mapping of entities onto or over the planet. While each individual planet does not have control of the scene, it helps the SolarSystem class create the any files for the models and entities relative to the planet. This allows us to isolate the information and entities that make up one planet from the others that may be in the SolarSystem scene file. [NoiseGen](class_noise_gen.html) Applies different Perlin noise functions to 2-dimensional images that are used to map to heightfields and decide where to place objects on and around the planet. We abstracted this code from our Plaet class, even though it is the only class to call it, since the NoiseGen functions are independent of any single planet nad is shared by all. We designed the Planet class to only contain information required for one planet in our Solar System Scene. [Mesh](class_mesh.html) defines methods to compute adjacency and normal information of the input Mesh, and methods to edit the Mesh. It is used by Planet, which passes in the vertex array and the tri array of faces of the original heightfields into Mesh. Planet then invokes the methods defined in Mesh to collapse mesh edges, to bevel the mesh, and to output it to [G3D::ArticulatedModel](http://g3d.cs.williams.edu/g3d/G3D10/build/manual/class_g3_d_1_1_articulated_model.html) or to OBJ files to be used in our scenes. Collapsing edges simplifies the mesh and makes the faces look less uniform which makes the low polyness more interesting. Bevelling smoothes the edges between faces and gives more of a polygon effect to the mesh. This design choice abstracts mesh editting from mesh creation and allowed us to to work on introducing low polyness separately from generating nice planets. [SolarSystem](class_solar_system.html) abstracts generating the Scene.Any file that includes our planet meshes. It defines separate methods to set up the lighting environment, write entities, write models, add and remove planet meshes from the scene. Defining this class was fundamental in cleaning up our code, since before SolarSystem we generated our whole Scene.Any in App. This abstraction allowed us to more easily and intuitively add features and polish our results.
